Summary
Automation needs were discussed following an automated card counter demonstration, contrasting current manual processes with a competitor's advanced system, for increased business scale.

Current Card Handling Automation
The current automation is limited to a card counter, which ensures sequential order for money-related cards and is used as a critical backup. The mandatory process of applying denomination stickers to cards remains entirely manual, particularly for Loblaw cards, creating significant labor issues.

Competitive Automation Capabilities Review
A competitor, Funstream, demonstrates advanced automation by scanning every card and applying a sequenced sticker tied to their inventory system, suggesting a technological lead of over 10 years. They noted that Funstream uses scanning equipment for inventory tracking, contrasting with their current manual recording of card numbers for stock management.

Scalability and Automation Challenges
Despite small margins, the company is experiencing high volume and growth, necessitating the move to the current office 2.5 years ago to manage massive inactive card shipments. The primary goal is finding an inexpensive Level 1 automation solution for denomination stickering to avoid the estimated $20,000 annual cost of manual labor while maintaining card sequential order.


Details
* Demonstration of the Automated Card Counter: Danny Norton requested a demonstration of the card counter, which is the only automation they currently use. Ivon demonstrated the machine, which can count a specific number of cards (e.g., 43 or 50) and ensures they remain in sequential order, which is critical because they are dealing with money (00:00:00). The counter is primarily used as a backup, and they often manually check the first and last card numbers to verify sequencing (00:00:57).
* Manual Sticker Application Process: The process for applying stickers to cards is entirely manual, using sticker guns. Stickers indicate different colors and denominations, and the application must be done by hand. Labeling every card is necessary unless the cards arrive pre-created by the merchant (00:01:46).
* Discussion of Competition's Automated Card Systems: Danny Norton showed a competitive product from Funstream, which utilizes QR codes and appears to have a mechanism to automate the process that ties into their back end (00:01:46). They noted that Funstream sends every single card through their machine to handle sequencing and inventory, a process they currently lack (00:02:50). Funstream’s machine is capable of reading the barcode, magstripe, or QR code and applying a sequenced sticker that ties into their inventory (00:10:00).
* Card Activation and Inventory Tracking: When they place an order for cards, they manually record the beginning and ending card numbers. In contrast, the competitor, Funstream, uses scanning equipment to record the first and last card of a shipment, which is tied into their inventory system. Cards are non-activated plastic until they are live and contain money, and Danny Norton shared examples of both active and inactive card inventory (00:03:59).
* Merchant Card Shipments and Stickering Problems: They primarily sell Walmart and Loblaw cards, preferring Walmart because their cards arrive pre-denominated, eliminating the need for stickering. Loblaw cards, however, arrive inactive and without denomination stickers, creating a significant labor issue for them (00:05:04). The manual stickering process is labor-intensive, particularly during high-volume periods like Christmas (00:06:03).
* Competitive Technology and Market Niche: Funstream, their top Canadian competitor, is estimated to be ten or more years ahead in technology and handles loyalty programs like RBC rewards (00:06:55). Their own company's biggest business is servicing food insecurity groups and First Nations groups out of Ontario, and despite being smaller than Funstream, they bought more Walmart cards in a recent year because Walmart is popular for food insecurity programs (00:07:53).
* Challenges with Finding Automated Stickering Solutions: Danny Norton discussed their search for a machine to automate card stickering, including assistance from an astrophysicist named James, who helped them use AI to find solutions (00:12:43). The primary difficulty in automation is finding a machine that can sticker a long box of cards while ensuring they remain in their original sequential order and shape (00:13:53) (00:16:22). The current goal is to find an inexpensive solution for "Level One" automation—simply applying a denomination sticker—to avoid the estimated $20,000 annual cost of manual stickering (00:15:16) (00:20:30).
* Card Denomination System and Inventory: They explained how card denominations are determined based on the two designs they have; one design is for 25s and 50s, and the other is for 100s and 250s (00:22:32). The inactive cards are stockpiled from various merchants, including Loblaw, and they only become "dead plastic" if the merchant goes out of business. They noted the importance of having cards like those from Tim Hortons and Subway readily available to meet time-sensitive orders, especially around Christmas (00:23:26).
* Invoice Processing and Client Discounts: Elena receives orders and determines the order number and applicable discounts, such as a 3% discount for non-profits that offsets the credit card fee, effectively selling cards at face value to them (00:24:30) (00:27:26). While they prefer orders from the website, older order forms can complicate the process, especially if clients order denominations they no longer carry (00:25:39).
* Sales Volume and Business Growth: The business operates on small margins but high volume, demonstrated by $146,000 invoiced in one morning and $1.5 million invoiced in one week (00:28:23). Sales volume increased substantially after the pre-COVID years of $13–14 million, jumping to $25 million during COVID and $30 million the year after (00:29:31). The increase was primarily driven by large orders from food insecurity groups, a shift from their fundraising arm, which previously required more work for smaller transactions (00:30:29).
* Infrastructure Changes and Inventory Scale: The business moved out of Danny Norton's home and into the current office two and a half years ago due to being too cluttered, primarily from storing large volumes of inactive cards (00:31:40). They receive massive shipments; for example, a Walmart order would be five times the size of a recent Loblaw order, necessitating the large space. The current stage requires improving technology to handle the continued volume and scale of the business (00:32:36).
