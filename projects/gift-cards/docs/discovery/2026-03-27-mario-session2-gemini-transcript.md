Summary
Digital gift card fulfillment processes were reviewed, focusing on the unique Walmart workflow and a desire for reduced manual effort.

Order Intake and Invoicing
The order process is initiated by the customer, then invoiced via QuickBooks by Mario, who uses a hard copy invoice as a reference for essential details. Fulfillment is strictly triggered only after the payment is confirmed by Elaine, who stamps and passes the paid invoice to Mario.

Walmart Fulfillment Walkthrough
The Walmart process is unique because it requires an additional step of using the Walmart back-end to generate card numbers before the internal software can create the final PDFs. The process is entirely just-in-time, requiring no existing card inventory, and generates sequential card numbers for internal monthly reconciliation with Walmart.

Process Pain Points and Risk
The digital fulfillment process contains many manual steps, including significant copying and pasting, which creates a high burden and reliance on the specific knowledge of a few personnel. There is a strong desire to streamline the process for smaller orders through a self-service option on the website to improve operational efficiency and reduce risk.


Details
* Gift Card Processing and Walmart's Unique Procedure: Danny Norton explained the general process for gift card fulfillment, noting that Elaine or others leave invoices that indicate which e-cards need processing. Walmart, however, uses a different process where the company allows them to generate the cards first, but payment is still required later. This process generates an Excel sheet for monthly reconciliation with Walmart, detailing all payments made (00:00:00).
* Invoice Matching and Card Fulfillment: The process relies on matching the work with the physical invoice. For instance, if an invoice indicates 150 cards, that's the number they fulfill. The Walmart process is noted as the only different one, with all others relying on the invoices left by Elaine (00:01:03).
* Walmart Card Generation and Financials: The initial step of generating cards through Walmart's back end is purely for their financial requirements and does not produce card numbers. Danny Norton confirmed they need a separate file to generate the actual card numbers. Danny Norton and Spencer Ririe agreed that examining the specific Walmart process is valuable because of this additional, different step (00:01:56).
* Petro Canada Inventory and Process Change: Petro Canada previously supplied PDF cards directly, which Lloyd converted into links. However, Petro Canada stopped this practice, requiring them to acquire cards from FunStream, which now only sends links (00:02:56). They still retain a small, older stock of $25 Petro Canada cards, but Danny Norton does not know the fulfillment process for this old stock, noting it is Lloyd's knowledge (00:03:52).
* Starting the Workday and Identifying New Orders: Spencer Ririe asked for clarification on how new orders are discovered and the initial computer steps taken when an order is received (00:04:56). Danny Norton explained that they do not rely on receiving an order PDF as Lloyd did (00:06:15).
* Order Flow and Internal Communication: Orders are placed by the customer through the website and go to Elaine, the administrator, who then notifies Mario that an order has been placed. Mario opens QuickBooks to create the invoice. After invoicing, they copy themself and Doug on the email if the order is digital, which serves as an early notification of a pending e-card task (00:06:15) (00:08:57).
* Invoicing Process in QuickBooks: For every new order, Elaine prints the information from the Formidable forms email and gives it to Mario (00:07:13). Mario then goes into QuickBooks, opens the customer's profile, and builds the invoice from scratch. The physical copy on the desk contains essential details such as the customer, merchant, quantity, and denomination (00:08:04).
* Digital Invoice Copying and Tracking: When a digital invoice is finalized, Mario copies themself and Doug on the email before sending it to the customer. They maintain an email folder specifically for e-cards, which contains all past and pending orders, aiding in organization. Spencer Ririe and Danny Norton decided to have Mario share their screen to walk through the invoicing process for better documentation (00:08:57) (00:11:41).
* Payment Confirmation and Fulfillment Trigger: The critical trigger for fulfillment is payment, not invoicing. Mario noted they keep the physical invoices in their office until payment is received (00:11:41). Elaine handles the payment processing, and once paid, they provide Mario with a copy of the invoice, usually marked with a "paid" stamp and sometimes a red "R" indicating reconciliation in QuickBooks (00:12:43).
* Process during Doug's Absence: When Doug is absent, Elaine manages Mario's responsibilities, keeping the file and passing the paid invoices to Mario for e-card fulfillment (00:13:45). Previously, when Lloyd handled the fulfillment, a copy of the digital invoice was sent to both Doug and Lloyd, and Doug would email Lloyd once payment was confirmed, resulting in a one-day delay (00:14:34).
* Order Intake Channels and Payment Necessity: Orders are received via order forms and the company's website. The fundamental rule is that nothing is triggered until payment is received, as they are selling money. While most corporate clients use the website, some grandfathered customers still use the old emailed order forms (00:16:28).
* Order Form Discrepancies and Inventory Issues: The website orders generally ensure the money component is correct, though taxes and provincial differences must be addressed during invoicing. The main problem with older order forms is that customers may request denominations or merchants (like HBC) that are no longer available (00:17:24). The invoice serves as the final confirmation, and the client is responsible for ensuring its correctness (00:18:48).
* Business Risk and Streamlining Digital Fulfillment: Danny Norton expressed a willingness to accept some risk to simplify the highly manual digital fulfillment process, aiming for a better and more streamlined experience for clients (00:22:58). Danny Norton highlighted that large digital orders, like one from the Edmonton Food Bank for $300,000 to $400,000, present challenges regarding helping clients distribute cards to end users and track redemption (00:23:50).
* Bulk Sales Definition and Customer Service: The large digital sales are considered bulk sales despite the challenge of delivering to individual end-users or reporting redemption status. Danny Norton maintained the importance of helping clients with fulfillment and reporting, viewing it as essential for meeting the needs of major clients and a key aspect of their customer service strength, distinguishing them from technology-heavy, slow-service competitors like FundStream (00:24:49) (00:33:24). Spencer Ririe concurred that losing a major customer over a service handoff, regardless of whether it is technically their responsibility, is a risk they should address (00:32:27).
* Walmart Integration and Card Number Access: For Walmart, the ability to check a balance depends on having the full 16-digit card number, which is known for digital cards (00:29:36). While the company is integrated with Walmart, other merchants like FundStream and M still limit the level of integration possible for financial and technological reasons (00:30:24).
* Walmart Fulfillment Process Demonstration (Step 1): Mario demonstrated the Walmart fulfillment process, noting a typical e-card order takes 15 to 30 minutes, or longer if there are many cards and merchants (00:42:20). Mario first downloads the invoice and moves it to a specific download folder in Google Drive (00:46:05). Mario then uses the PowerShell to run a script against the physical invoice on the desk, ensuring they are working on the correct order (00:47:13).
* Walmart Fulfillment Process Demonstration (Step 2): The PowerShell successfully executes and creates an Excel spreadsheet, which is stored in the Walmart folder (00:48:02). For Walmart, an additional step is required: inputting the information into a specific template Excel sheet for the Walmart back end to generate the card numbers (00:49:16). This process is purely "just in time" and requires no existing card inventory (00:51:26).
* Walmart Fulfillment Process Demonstration (Step 3): Mario updates the template with the correct card value and copies the line for the required quantity (100 cards in this case) (00:52:13). They then use the "Walmart virtual GC activation" software to open the specially organized file (00:53:11). The software runs, and upon success, generates two new documents, including an updated Excel sheet containing the card numbers and PINs (00:54:19).
* Internal Tracking and Card Delivery: Mario also updates a separate internal monthly sheet to track all activations, ensuring that Doug pays Walmart the correct amount at the end of the month. Mario noted the card numbers generated are sequential (00:58:07). The output is not limited to a PDF or URL; customers can receive the card via URL or print out the PDF, allowing them to choose their preferred method of distribution (00:44:13).
* Walmart Physical Card Activation Process: Walmart has mandated that senior management no longer wants manual card activations, creating a process challenge due to the high volume of physical cards sold. Danny Norton and Lloyd are planning a call with the Ferve and Walmart teams to gain access for activation using first and last numbers, as activating card by card is impractical due to the volume (00:59:07). The physical card invoice details the cards activated, their denominations, and the amount owed to Walmart for the daily sales (01:04:02).
* Monthly Reporting to Walmart: At the end of each month, a report summarizing all activated cards, their denominations, and the amount due to Walmart is compiled. The team's process involves sending a completed sheet of activated cards to a contact, who typically confirms activation within a few hours, a process that is not done manually (01:00:21).
* E-Card Generation Workflow Initiation: The process of generating e-cards starts with copying card numbers generated by the PowerShell script and pasting them into the correct template. The team maintains two different spreadsheets: one is an internal copy, and the other is used for generating the card via the Walmart system (01:01:18).
* Transforming Card Numbers into PDFs: Following the process used for Amazon cards, the team transforms the card numbers into PDFs using the appropriate software. The system reads the card numbers from the tab generated from the invoice, specifically processing gift cards across different sheets and denominations like $25 and $50 (01:02:14).
* Handling Different Card Denominations: The software is designed to process all denominations across different tabs, including $25, $50, $100, and a "variable" tab for all other amounts. The software automatically runs through all tabs and generates PDFs for those with card information, like the $50 cards in the current example (01:03:14).
* Volume of Physical Card Orders: The number of cards ordered, especially for physical cards around Christmas time, is too large to handle one by one, necessitating the streamlined activation process (01:04:02). During peak season (mid-November to December), orders are placed with Walmart daily, contrasting with the twice-weekly activation schedule during the rest of the year (01:04:50).
* Validation and File Organization: After PDF generation, a manual validation step is typically performed for safety, though it does not take long. All generated files are then moved into the prototype file structure, where they are identified by a specific numerical sequence for easy location and handling (01:05:45).
* Walmart E-Card Features and Customer Delivery: A Walmart e-card includes the design, denomination, barcode, card number, PIN, and usage instructions. For one specific customer, the delivery format required a password-encrypted Zip file containing individual PDFs instead of a URL (01:06:40).
* Encryption Process for Individual PDFs: To fulfill the customer's request, an encryption step is applied to each individual PDF using a software developed by Lloyd. The password for the encryption is always the invoice number without the "TP" prefix and the dash (e.g., 104152), regardless of whether a URL or PDF is sent (01:07:41) (01:10:48).
* Final Steps and Sending Digital Cards: The encrypted Zip file is extracted, the original unencrypted folder is deleted, and the individual files are now password-protected (01:08:38) (01:10:48). The final step involves sending the cards to the customer in an initial email containing the password-protected Zip file and a PDF copy of the invoice, followed by a separate email containing the password (01:11:56) (01:14:22).
* Post-Delivery Reconciliation and Finalization: After sending the cards, manual notes are added to the hard copy of the invoice, documenting the time and date of the shipment and the person who handled the process (01:14:22). Elena handles the final payment process and files the physical invoice away (01:15:30).
* Vendor Processes and Edge Cases: The core process for digital card fulfillment is similar for most vendors, generally starting with a PowerShell script to generate an Excel file (01:17:12). Walmart is the exception, as they generate their own numbers, but for most vendors, the process involves copying and pasting card numbers to generate PDFs (01:18:06).
* Amazon Card Inventory Handling: Upon receiving a new Amazon order, the encrypted file is saved, the password is removed, and the cards are transferred to the Amazon inventory folder on Google Drive (01:19:03). The file may contain multiple denominations, depending on the order and the merchant (01:20:17).
* E-Card Process Workflow Exceptions: Outside of Walmart, the main workflows are either receiving pre-generated cards (like URLs or PDFs) directly from the merchant or receiving card numbers that require the internal software to generate the final PDFs (01:22:32). For merchants like Tim Hortons, the customer is provided with information, including a challenge code, to access their card via a dedicated link, which simplifies the fulfillment process for the team (01:18:06).
* Order Volume and Timing: Mario typically sends out one to three digital orders per day, increasing to about five to ten per day during peak seasons, such as November, December, and the busy March period due to near-government agency year-ends (01:34:51). The time to process a typical order for digital delivery is short, generally five to ten minutes, with ten minutes being the worst-case scenario (01:38:35).
* Workflow Pain Points: The biggest pain points identified in the digital card workflow are the amount of required manual copying and pasting, and the high attention to detail required when dealing with different customer groups and organizations (01:41:48). The extensive manual steps involved in generation and email preparation are considered burdensome, especially for recurring, smaller orders (01:42:47).
* Desire for Self-Service Functionality: There is a preference for a self-service option on the website for smaller digital orders, allowing customers to automatically receive cards via email, thus removing the manual fulfillment burden from the team. Bulk orders would still be handled internally, but streamlining the smaller orders would significantly improve operational efficiency (01:44:00).
* Need for Process Documentation and System Reliance: The current process relies heavily on the specific knowledge of Mario and Lloyd, creating a significant risk because no one else in the office currently knows how to handle digital card fulfillment (01:47:59). There is an urgent need to streamline the process, making it easier to train new personnel quickly and reduce reliance on a single person (01:49:49).
* Physical Card Stickering Future Strategy: Future plans for the physical card process involve a three-level strategy: first, automating the basic stickering of cards while maintaining sequential order. Second, implementing a QR code on the plastic card that links to inventory (01:55:46). Third, integrating that system with merchants and processors (01:55:46).
